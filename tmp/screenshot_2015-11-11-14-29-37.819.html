AbstractAbstract

Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (Rubisco) catalyses the conversion of atmospheric carbon dioxide into organic compounds in photosynthetic organisms. Alongside carboxylating the five-carbon sugar ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP)1,​2,​3, Rubisco produces a small amount of xylulose-1,5-bisphosphate (XuBP), a potent inhibitor of Rubisco4. The AAA+ protein Rubisco activase removes XuBP from the active site of Rubisco in an ATP-dependent process5,6. However, free XuBP rapidly rebinds to Rubisco, perpetuating its inhibitory effect. Here, we combine biochemical and structural analyses to show that the CbbY protein of the photosynthetic bacterium Rhodobacter sphaeroides and Arabidopsis thaliana is a highly selective XuBP phosphatase. We also show that CbbY converts XuBP to the non-inhibitory compound xylulose-5-phosphate, which is recycled back to RuBP. We solve the crystal structures of CbbY from R. sphaeroides and A. thaliana, and through mutational analysis show that the cap domain of the protein confers the selectivity for XuBP over RuBP. Finally, in vitro experiments with CbbY from R. sphaeroides reveal that CbbY cooperates with Rubisco activase to prevent a detrimental build-up of XuBP at the Rubisco active site. We suggest that CbbY, which is conserved in algae and plants, is an important component of the cellular machinery that has evolved to deal with the shortcomings of the ancient enzyme Rubisco.

The cbbY gene of the photolithoautotrophic bacterium R. sphaeroides is located in the cbb operon together with the genes encoding the large and small subunits of form I Rubisco (cbbL; cbbS), Rubisco activase (cbbX) and phosphoglycolate phosphatase (cbbZ)7 (Fig. 1a). Whereas deletion of cbbZ alone increased Rubisco specific activity by ∼20% under photoautotrophic growth conditions, the combined deletion of ccbY and cbbZ resulted in a ∼10% reduction in specific Rubisco activity compared with wild type7. This suggested a function of the CbbY protein in the Calvin–Benson–Bassham (CBB) cycle. A Blast analysis identified the cbbY gene product as a member of the HAD2 clan of the haloacid dehalogenase (HAD) hydrolase superfamily8. These enzymes include phosphatases, β-phosphoglucomutases and dehalogenases with Mg as a cofactor. They are involved in a variety of cellular processes ranging from amino acid biosynthesis to detoxification8. The genomes of photosynthetic organisms from bacteria to plants all contain CbbY homologues (Supplementary Fig. 1). The A. thaliana homologue AT3G48420 is abundant in chloroplast stroma9,10.

The recombinantly expressed and purified CbbY proteins from R. sphaeroides (RsCbbY) and A. thaliana (AtCbbY) exhibited phosphatase activity towards XuBP and RuBP. Only one equivalent of phosphate per sugar molecule was released (Fig. 1b). Moreover, CbbY was able to release one phosphate equivalent from fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (FBP) but no phosphate release was observed with fructose-6-phosphate (F6P) or ribulose-5-phosphate (Ru5P) (Supplementary Fig. 2a–c). This suggests that CbbY hydrolyses the 1-phosphate of these sugars (Supplementary Fig. 2d). Both CbbY proteins exhibited Michaelis–Menten kinetics with respect to RuBP and XuBP, but the Km for XuBP (∼30 µM) was ∼100-fold lower than that for RuBP (∼3.0 mM) and ∼200-fold lower than that for FBP (∼5.7 mM) (Fig. 1c,d and Supplementary Tables 1,2), indicating selectivity for XuBP as the substrate. The turnover number (kcat) for XuBP (∼2.4 s−1) was ∼50-fold higher than that for RuBP (∼0.045 s−1) (Supplementary Tables 1 and 2). Thus, the catalytic efficiency (kcat/Km) with XuBP is approximately four orders of magnitude higher, suggesting that CbbY can effectively prevent the inhibitory build up of XuBP in the presence of physiological concentrations of RuBP in the millimolar range11. Interestingly, the phosphatase that degrades the night-time Rubisco inhibitor 2-carboxy-D-arabinitol 1-phosphate (CA1P) in some plants12 is evolutionarily unrelated to CbbY.

To understand the molecular basis for the substrate selectivity of CbbY, we solved the crystal structures of RsCbbY and AtCbbY. The structure of Mg-free RsCbbY was initially solved by mercury-single isomorphous replacement with anomalous scattering (Hg-SIRAS) at 1.9 Å resolution. The resulting model was used as a search template to solve by molecular replacement the structures of RsCbbY and AtCbbY in the presence of Mg at 1.2 Å and 1.3 Å resolution, respectively (Fig. 2a,b and Supplementary Fig. 3 and Supplementary Table 3). Like other HAD family members, such as β-phosphoglucomutase (β-PGM), CbbY has a two-domain structure: a core domain (residues 1–14 and 93–230; numbering for RsCbbY) harbouring the catalytic residues and a flexibly attached cap domain (residues 15–92)8. The active site pocket is located at the interface between the core and cap domains, which are linked by a flexible hinge region. The structures of RsCbbY and AtCbbY revealed closed and open conformations of the cap domain, respectively, with a phosphate from the precipitant being bound in RsCbbY (Fig. 2a and  2b). In the open conformation of AtCbbY, the cap domain displays a rigid-body rotation of 42° relative to the core domain, providing access to the substrate binding site. A comparison of the closed structure of RsCbbY with that of β-PGM, which catalyses the conversion of glucose-1P to glucose-6P, suggests that the geometry of the catalytic residues in the core domain is conserved in CbbY13,​14,​15 (Fig. 2c and Supplementary Fig. 4). In β-PGM, Asp8 and Asp170 (Asp8 and Asp176 in RsCbbY) coordinate the Mg (Fig. 2d). Lys145 (Lys151 in RsCbbY) together with Mg activates and orients Asp8 (Asp8 in RsCbbY) for nucleophilic attack on glucose-1P (ref. 16) and Asp10 (Asp10 in RsCbbY) acts as a general acid for the formation of the hydroxyl group on the sugar14. In CbbY, Thr116 is oriented for assisting hydrolysis of the phospho-Asp8 intermediate, whereas β-PGM has an Ala residue at this position, conserving the phosphoryl group for transfer to the 6-OH group in glucose (Supplementary Fig. 4).

To understand how CbbY achieves specificity for XuBP, we mutated the conserved Asp10 to Asn, thereby trapping XuBP in the active site. RsCbbY(D10N) was phosphatase inactive (Supplementary Table 1) and was crystallized in the closed conformation with XuBP in the active site (Fig. 3a and Supplementary Table 3). Four independent copies of the complex in the crystal lattices were virtually identical suggesting that crystal packing does not have a significant impact on the protein conformation (Supplementary Fig. 3). As revealed in the 1.3 Å resolution structure, the bound XuBP assumes a bent conformation and makes extensive contacts with the core and cap domains (Fig. 3a,b). The residues of the binding pocket that contact XuBP are highly conserved in CbbY proteins (Supplementary Fig. 1). The 1-phosphate group of XuBP is coordinated to the catalytic Mg in the core domain (Fig. 3c,d), consistent with the finding that only the 1-phosphate of the sugar substrate is hydrolysed (Fig. 1b). Whereas the catalytic residues in the core domain are generally conserved in the HAD superfamily, the residues in the cap domain of CbbY are distinct from other HAD members, including β-PGM (Supplementary Fig. 1). The cap domain of CbbY homologues share a conserved signature motif, comprising Glu17, His20, Phe24, Tyr42, Gly50, Gly51, Lys52, Glu53, Arg54, His75 and Lys78 (Fig. 3d and Supplementary Fig. 1). His20, His75 and Lys78 are oriented to dissipate the negative charge of the 5-phosphate group (Fig. 3b,d). Tyr42 positions Glu17 and Arg54 towards the ligand, such that the guanidine group of Arg54 and the 4-hydroxyl group coordinate the 2-carbonyl and 4-hydroxyl groups of XuBP, which is additionally joined by a hydrogen bond to Glu17. The 3-hydroxyl group of XuBP is joined by a hydrogen bond to Gly50 and the 3-hydroxymethylene moiety is sandwiched between the backbones at residues Gly50 in the cap domain and Thr116 in the core domain (Fig. 3b,c,d). These interactions appear to be critical in stabilizing the bent conformation of the bound XuBP (Fig. 3a,b). Notably, XuBP and RuBP differ only by their stereochemistry at the C3 position (Supplementary Fig. 2d) and binding of RuBP in the same conformation as XuBP would cause a steric clash with the backbone at Thr116 and Thr117 (Supplementary Fig. 5a,b). Thus binding of RuBP requires rearrangements that would probably disturb the geometry of the catalytic residues. The curvature of the active site pocket steric ally compromises the binding of 2,3-pentodiulose-1,5-bisphosphate (PDBP) and 3-ketoarabinitol-1,5-bisphosphate (KABP), two other misfire products of Rubisco (Supplementary Fig. 5c,d).

We next investigated the functional role of the cap domain signature motif by mutational analysis. Mutation of Glu17, His20, Tyr42 and Lys78 (Glu91, His95, Tyr117 and Lys161 in AtCbbY) to alanine reduced the phosphatase activity of CbbY with XuBP as the substrate by ∼40–80% and increased the Km up to 8.5-fold, lowering the catalytic efficiency ∼10- to 35-fold (Supplementary Fig. 5e and Supplementary Tables 1 and 2). The cap mutation R54A (R129A in AtCbbY) reduced phosphatase activity with XuBP by ∼97% (Supplementary Tables 1 and 2), consistent with a critical role of Arg54 in enforcing the bent conformation of XuBP and positioning the 1-phosphate for efficient hydrolysis (Fig. 3b,c,d). Remarkably, none of these mutations had a significant effect on the activity of CbbY with RuBP as the substrate, indicating that the cap domain confers the selectivity for XuBP over RuBP (Supplementary Fig. 5e and Supplementary Tables 1,2).

How would CbbY contribute to maintaining Rubisco activity? XuBP is formed at a much higher rate than other misfire products of Rubisco and thus would build up rapidly during active photosynthesis4. We determined a Km value for RuBP of ∼15.7 µM for RsRubisco (Supplementary Fig. 6a), consistent with previous analysis17. By measuring the initial rates of Rubisco activity in the presence of increasing concentrations of XuBP, we confirmed that XuBP is a potent competitive inhibitor of RsRubisco with a Ki value of ∼1.3 µM (Supplementary Fig. 6a,b). A similar Ki value of ∼4.8 µM was determined for tobacco Rubisco4. Moreover, XuBP is only slowly released from inactive Rubisco that either has not yet undergone activation by carbamylation of the active site lysine18 or has been decarbamylated during catalysis19,20.

Non-carbamylated Rubisco with bound XuBP (E.XuBP) was enzymatically inactive and essentially remained inactive in the presence RsCbbY and RuBP (Fig. 4a). In the presence of the AAA+ protein Rubisco activase (RsRca; gene cbbX in the cbb operon, see Fig. 1a) and ATP, E.XuBP was partially reactivated (Fig. 4a). However, full activity was only reached when CbbY was added together with RsRca/ATP. This suggests that XuBP was released by RsRca and hydrolysed by CbbY, thereby preventing it from rebinding to Rubisco and acting as a competitive inhibitor (Fig. 4a). Thus, RsRca and CbbY cooperate in reactivating the E.XuBP complex and maintaining Rubisco activity. It is interesting to note in this context that the chemoautotrophic bacterium Alcaligenes eutrophus contains two operons in which the cbbX and cbbY genes are linked and are positioned immediately downstream from Rubisco coding sequences, whereas cbbZ is located much further downstream within the cbb gene cluster21,22.

Although Rubisco can carboxylate XuBP, producing two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate, this reaction is far too slow to explain effective clearance of the inhibitor4. Here we have identified a selective sugar phosphatase, CbbY, present in plants, green and red algae, as well as many photosynthetic bacteria, that hydrolyses this inhibitory misfire product of Rubisco to the productive CBB cycle intermediate Xu5P (Fig. 4b). CbbY, a member of the HAD hydrolase superfamily, has high selectivity for XuBP over RuBP and other sugars, allowing it to hydrolyse small amounts of XuBP (μM) in the presence of physiologically high concentrations of RuBP (mM). The substrate specificity of CbbY is remarkable given the structural similarity between XuBP and RuBP, which differ only by their stereochemistry at the C3 position. The ∼6000-fold preference for XuBP over RuBP is achieved by adapting residues in the cap domain of CbbY to function in positioning XuBP for effective hydrolysis of the 1-phosphate by the catalytic residues in the core domain. It is interesting to note that CbbY adds to the complexity of cellular machineries, including chaperones for folding, assembly and reactivation3, that have evolved to deal with the shortcomings of the ancient enzyme Rubisco.

MethodsMethods

Xylulose-1,5-bisphosphate (XuBP) synthesis

XuBP was prepared by a aldolase-catalyzed aldol addition reaction of dihydroxyacetone phosphate and glycolaldehyde phosphate. Glycolaldehyde phosphate was synthesized by periodate oxidation of D,L-glycerol phosphate23 (see Supplementary Methods for detailed procedure).

Plasmids, protein expression and purification

Expression and purification of Rubisco and Rubisco activase (Rca) from R. sphaeroides were as described24. The open reading frame of CbbY from R. sphaeroides (RsCbbY) was amplified from genomic DNA and cloned between the SacII/HindIII restriction sites of the pHue plasmid25, yielding pHueRsCbbY encoding a His6-tagged ubiquitin fusion protein. Similarly, the sequence encoding the predicted chloroplastic CbbY from A. thaliana, residues 74–319, was amplified from cDNA and cloned into pHue using the SacII/NotI restriction sites, yielding pHueAtCbbY. The coding regions of all constructs were verified by DNA sequencing. See Supplementary Methods for purification of His6-tagged ubiquitin fusion proteins.

Enzymatic assays

All assays were performed at 25 °C and are described in detail in the Supplementary Methods. CbbY activity was measured by monitoring the release of inorganic phosphate from XuBP or other sugar phosphates using colorimetric detection of phosphate with Malachite green reagent26. Rubisco carboxylation activity (0.1 µM holoenzyme) was measured as previously described24. Initial rate constants were determined by linear regression. The parameters kcat and Km were determined by non-linear fitting of the rate data versus initial RuBP concentrations using the program Origin.

To analyse competitive inhibition of Rubisco by XuBP, Rubisco reactions were performed in the presence of increasing concentrations of XuBP at each concentration of RuBP (Supplementary Fig. 6a,b). The apparent Michaelis–Menten constant Kapp was determined as above. The inhibition constant Ki was determined by linear expression analysis using the formula: (Kapp/Km)−1=[I]/Kiwhere [I] is concentration of the inhibitor.

Rubisco reactivation by RsRca was measured as previously described with some modifications24,27 (see Supplementary Methods).

Crystallization and data collection

Crystals of apo-RsCbbY in space group I4 grew at 20 °C in a hanging-drop set-up at a concentration of 10 mg ml−1 RsCbbY using 16–20% PEG-6000 and 0.1 M Hepes-NaOH pH 7.0 as a precipitant. Crystals of the RsCbbY·phosphate complex were obtained after incubation with 10 mM fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, 22% PEG-6000 and 0.1 M MES-HCl pH 6.0 as precipitant. Crystals of RsCbbY(D10N) in complex with XuBP were obtained at 18 °C with the PEG Suite (Qiagen) conditions A10 and A11 (crystal form II, 25% PEG-1000, 0.1 M MES-NaOH pH 6.5; crystal form I, 25% PEG-550MME, 0.1 M MES-NaOH pH 6.5). Crystals of AtCbbY were obtained at 4 °C with 25% (w/v) PEG-6000 and 0.1 M Tris-HCl pH 8.5.

Diffraction data were collected at beamlines X10SA and X06DA at SLS Villigen, Switzerland and ID23-1 at ESRF Grenoble, France (see Supplementary Methods for structure solution).

Accession codes

The coordinates and the structure factors of the various CbbY proteins crystallized have been deposited in the Protein Data Bank under the accession codes 4UAR, 4UAS, 4UAT, 4UAU, and 4UAV.
